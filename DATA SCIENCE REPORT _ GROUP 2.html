  1. INTRODUCTION

This data report aims to analyze the difference in traffic flow for each type of transportation in two months, focusing on the busiest periods of the day or month. The dataset includes information such as time, date, day of the week, types of transportation (such as car, bike, bus, truck), total, and traffic situation. The business aims for this dataset is to understand if traffic volume becomes abnormal during a fixed period in a day, enabling them to develop plans to improve traffic conditions during peak hours.

The descriptive analytics will explain the correlation between traffic and vehicle numbers, or the average number of vehicles per day of the week. Visualizations like total number of vehicles moving per hour, trends in which days of the week have the highest traffic volume, allowing the team to detect which vehicles, which hours will affect the traffic situation.

By using the combination of several statistical models and machine learning techniques, this report tries to answer some of the fundamental business questions with predicting the traffic situation accurately by comparing two Logistics Regression models with K-Nearest Neighbors, which is used as a classifier. In addition, we also used Linear Regression model to predict total traffic volume based on the number of each type of vehicle and time, using Total as the dependent variable and variables such as CarCount, BikeCount, BusCount, TruckCount, Hour, Day of the week as independent variables.

  1. DATA SOURCES

For this particular project the data is sourced from a dataset provided by Hasibullah Aman on Kaggle. The Dataset includes two files: Traffic.csv and TrafficTwoMonth.csv, we choose TrafficTwoMonth.csv because the data is updated in more detail than the Traffic.csv file: Contains Time, Date, Day of the week, CarCount, BikeCount, BusCount, TruckCount, Total, Traffic Situation, which give us a snapshot of the number of vehicles and traffic conditions over time.

2.1. Dataset Overview

This dataset is a valuable resource for studying traffic conditions as it contains information collected by a computer vision model. The model detects four classes of vehicles: cars, bikes, buses, and trucks. The dataset is stored in a CSV file and includes additional columns such as time in hours, date, days of the week, and counts for each vehicle type (CarCount, BikeCount, BusCount, TruckCount). The "Total" column represents the total count of all vehicle types detected within a 15-minute duration.

The dataset is updated every 15 minutes, providing a comprehensive view of traffic patterns over the course of one month. Additionally, the dataset includes a column indicating the traffic situation categorized into four classes: 1-Heavy, 2-High, 3-Normal, and 4-Low. This information can help assess the severity of congestion and monitor traffic conditions at different times and days of the week.

2.2. Usefulness of the data set

The dataset is useful in transportation planning, congestion management, and traffic flow analysis. It helps understand vehicle demand, identify congested areas, and inform infrastructure improvements. The dataset enables targeted interventions like signal optimizations and lane adjustments. It allows researchers to study traffic patterns by hour, day, or specific dates and explore correlations with external factors. It supports transportation research on vehicle relationships and traffic behavior. Urban planners can assess traffic impact for zoning and infrastructure decisions. Overall, the dataset empowers stakeholders to make data-driven decisions, enhance urban mobility, and create efficient and sustainable cities.

  1.  DATA PREPARATION AND CLEANING

Data cleaning is one of the most crucial steps in the data preparation process to serve analysis and ensure the most accurate results. First, we thoroughly examined the structure, data types, and completeness of the data by checking the summarized statistics, types, and sample rows. The dataset contains 5,952 entries with 9 columns, including categorical columns (such as "time," "day of the week," and "traffic situation") and numerical columns (such as "Total," "TruckCount," "BusCount," "BikeCount," "CarCount," and "date"). This initial check shows that there is no missing data, which helps demonstrate that this is a complete dataset and eliminates the need for imputation.  

Throughout the process of analyzing, compiling statistics, and summarizing the key points about the distribution of the data, it has been found. columns such as "bikecount," "buscount," and "truckcount" have the smallest number of occurrences in a day, reaching zero. Meanwhile, based on the data in the "carcount" column, it is recorded that there are at least 5 vehicles. This proves one thing: the number of vehicles on the road during any 15-minute period will always be greater than or equal to 5. Moreover, the "carcount" also yields a significantly higher average than the other columns, with 65.4407, while the averages of the other vehicle columns such as "bike count," "Buscount," and "Truckcount" are 12.1615, 12.913, and 18.6463, respectively.

To prepare the data for analysis and modeling, several transformations were used: Time columns were converted to numeric representations for time-based analysis; Day of the week were converted to one-hot encoded columns for incorporating categorical data into numerical models. This transformation enables categorical values ​​to be used in regression and classification algorithms without introducing unintended ordinal relationships.

The dataset was split into training and testing sets to ensure the reliability of the machine learning models. We used the train_test_split function from the scikit-learn library. Apply a split ratio of 80% of the data for training and 20% for testing. Then predict and calculate the accuracy on the training and testing sets. This method helps avoid overfitting and ensures a reliable evaluation of the model performance.

  1. PREDICTIVE METHODS

5.1.  Logistic regression

-  Classification Report:

The logistic regression model achieved an accuracy of 0.87, or 87%, in both the training and testing sets, indicating that the model is stable and shows no signs of serious overfitting or underfitting. However, when analyzing each class in detail in the report, we see a few important points as follows:

Heavy class: Precision = 0.92, Recall = 0.97, is the class with the best results. The model almost never misses heavy traffic and rarely makes wrong predictions to other classes. High class (high traffic): This is the most difficult class to classify with a Precision of only 0.61 and a Recall of 0.47.

Low class: Precision = 0.74 and Recall = 0.75. Although this result is quite good, the model still has some significant confusion, mainly with the low class. Normal class: Precision = 0.90 and Recall = 0.91. With this result, the model is very effective in recognizing normal traffic.

That is, nearly 53% of the high cases were mistakenly predicted by the model to be another class, especially normal. So, the model performs very well for the heavy and normal conditions but has difficulty separating the high and low conditions.

  • Confusion Matrix:

The following is a clear illustration of how the classes are anticipated when looking at the confusion matrix. Heavy class: 209 out of 216 instances were properly predicted; only 6 cases were mislabeled as high, and 1 as normal. This is an excellent outcome that demonstrates how well the model detects the "heavy traffic" scenario. Normal class is 680 out of 748 cases were correctly classified; however, 18 instances were misclassified as high and 39 cases as low. Although it is a little inaccurate, it is nonetheless apparent.

Low class: Only 109/146 cases were correctly predicted, while 37 cases were mistaken for normal. This shows that the model has difficulty distinguishing between low and normal conditions. High class only had 38/81 cases that were correctly predicted. Almost half of these cases were mistaken for normal (35 cases), showing that high traffic conditions are much more difficult to recognize.

Therefore, low-normal and high-normal errors are two major problems that need to be addressed. More data collection or model improvements may be needed to clearly separate these conditions.

  • Feature importance:

The feature importance graph shows that TruckCount and BusCount are the two factors that have the greatest influence on the prediction results. This can be explained by the fact that trucks and buses often add to traffic congestion due to their size and slow speed. Next, CarCount also plays an important role because cars account for a large proportion of the traffic flow. In addition, Day of week and hour also play a significant role, clearly reflecting the traffic trend changing according to rush hour and working day of the week. Features such as minute and Date have a lower role in prediction, indicating that they are less decisive in this logistic regression model

The results from this model can bring many benefits in practice, especially in traffic management and warning of congestion. Based on the model's predictions, authorities can identify in advance the time frames or areas with heavy or high traffic conditions. Measures such as banning trucks during rush hours or adjusting bus operating times can be implemented to reduce congestion. Applying the results to a traffic forecasting system can help people avoid areas and times with potential traffic congestion, thereby saving travel time. Information from important features such as TruckCount and BusCount can help plan additional routes for trucks and buses, or build separate lanes.

The logistic regression model has achieved good results, especially in identifying heavy and normal traffic conditions. However, there is still confusion between the low and high classes and the normal class. Features such as TruckCount, BusCount, and CarCount play a key role in helping the model recognize traffic conditions.

5.2. K-Nearest Neighbors

At the beginning, we have to clarify the meaning of each axis. For the X-axis, it represents the values of K, which means the numbers of neighbors in the KNN algorithm. In this situation, the values of K are from 1 to 20. Besides, the Y-axis represents the accuracy of the KNN model.

The accuracy of a model is influenced by the K values, with a peak at 0.885 and a gradual decrease from 1 to 5. K values above 8 show a downward trend, with the lowest at 0.76. Small K values may overfit the training data, while high K values may result in underfitting and decreased accuracy. The KNN table shows that the highest accuracy is achieved with K values between 4 and 7.


As the result that has been observed, selecting K between 4 and 7 will assist balance between overfitting and underfitting with this data. Using the KNN algorithm, it can help issue warnings or identify unusual traffic conditions.

  • Classification report:

The K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) model, which the classification report showed, is an important tool for evaluating a classification model's performance. This report includes important information for each class, from class 0 to class 3, such as precision, recall, F1-score, and support.

Class 0: The model performs extremely well for this class, with the accuracy exceeding 90%, showing that it is highly effective at correctly categorizing items in this class. Class 1: The model's performance for class 1 is not as high as other classes. The accuracy and sensitivity are both lower, especially the sensitivity which only reaches 0.63. This may be due to the characteristics of class 1 being difficult to classify or the data being insufficiently balanced.

Class 2: With all measures over 0.75, the model performs admirably in class 2. This suggests that the model can classify this class with a fair degree of accuracy. Class 3: With all indicators above 0.90, the model does exceptionally well for grade 3. A high degree of support also suggests that the class's data is quite comprehensive and aids in the model's efficient learning.

Class 1 performed worse than classes 0 and 3, which may have been caused by complicated features or unbalanced data. The classification performance for this class may be improved by improving the training data or modifying the model parameters.

  • Confusion matrix:

The confusion matrix is a summary of a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) classification model's performance, dividing it into "heavy," "high," "low," and "normal" classes. It helps identify areas for improvement and predicts specific classes. The "heavy" class has high accuracy, but some cases misclassify into "high" and "normal" due to small feature similarities. The "high" class has respectable accuracy, but a significant portion misclassified data as "normal."

The model performs well, with high accuracy for normal and low classifications. However, most misclassifications occur between heavy and normal groups, requiring better feature differentiation or data preprocessing. By computing F1-scores for every class, accuracy and recall can be enhanced, offering a more thorough assessment of the model's performance and pinpointing areas in need of development.

  • Feature Importance:

The table and chart make it obvious how important the characteristics are. The two most significant features in this KNN model are the overall number of vehicles and the number of cars. 5.3. Comparison of Logistic Regression and KNN models

5.3.1. Compare Precision, Recall and F1-score indexes

Precision index: In the table above, we can see that the Precision index of KNN is 0.88, while that of Logistic Regression is 0.87. This shows that the KNN model has a slightly higher accuracy in predicting traffic conditions (such as low, normal, heavy, high) compared to Logistic Regression.

Recall index: Through the comparison table, we see that the Recall index of KNN is still higher than that of Logistic Regression (0.88 and 0.87, respectively). This shows us that the KNN model detects more true cases and misses fewer true positive samples.

F1-score: As the table, we can see that a higher F1-Score indicates a better balance in the model. Through the comparison table, we can see that the F1-Score of KNN is 0.89, slightly higher than that of Logistic Regression at 0.88. Thus, the KNN model has a better balance between Recall and Precision; therefore, the KNN model is also more effective in the generalization process.

To conclude, KNN outperforms Logistic Regression in predicting traffic conditions based on Precision, Recall, and F1-Score. This is due to its non-linear nature, making it suitable for complex data like traffic situations where feature-label relationships are not logistic. Logistic Regression potentially overlooking complex relationships in the data.

5.3.2. Comparing the Confusion Matrix of the two models

We can see a clear difference in the prediction performance of each class: KNN predicts low, high and normal classes more accurately; while Logistic predicts heavy class a little more accurately, showing that this model has difficulty distinguishing the boundary between these two classes.

Overall, both models are capable of predicting traffic situations relatively accurately based on the input features. However, KNN is the better choice when the model is required to be able to recognize rare and difficult-to-classify traffic situations such as high and low. Logistic Regression is more stable in the obvious and common classes. This result proves that, with the existing dataset and features, predicting traffic situations based on features such as number of vehicles and time of day is completely feasible.

5.3.3 Comparison of Feature Importance:

Top Feature Differences: In the Logistic Regression model, TruckCount holds the highest importance, indicating the significant influence of individual vehicle counts on predictions. On the other hand, time predominates in the KNN model, suggesting that temporal factors are more crucial for forecasting traffic patterns.

Vehicle Counts: Logistic Regression ranks other vehicle counts like BusCount, BikeCount and CarCount relatively higher, emphasizing a broader consideration of traffic composition. In KNN model, these vehicle counts contribute less to the model, indicating a potential limitation in capturing the complexity of traffic data.

Temporal Features: Both models recognize the importance of hour and day_of_week, but Date, time stands out in KNN model, showcasing its relevance for traffic analysis over raw vehicle counts.

5.4. Linear Regression

In this analysis, we built a linear regression model to predict the Total Vehicle Count (Total) based on the input factors including the number of cars (CarCount), motorcycles (BikeCount), buses (BusCount) and trucks (TruckCount). The results from the model show a very high performance with the value R-squared = 1,000, Adj. R-squared =1,000, meaning that 100% of the variation in the total vehicle count is explained by the input variables. This is an ideal result, reflecting that the relationship between total traffic and the components is completely linear.

The F-statistic value is very large ( 9.013 × 1 0 31 9.013×10 31 ) and the p-value is 0, confirming that the input variables are statistically significant and play an important role in the model. When analyzing the regression coefficients, all coefficients of CarCount, BikeCount, BusCount, and TruckCount are equal to 1.000, which means that each vehicle in each group contributes exactly one unit to the total traffic. This is consistent with the logic that the total traffic is calculated by directly adding the components.

  • Residuals vs Fitted Values:

The Residuals vs Fitted Values ​​plot provides insight into the accuracy of the model as well as potential patterns or problems in the predictions. In this chart below, we can see that most of the residuals at low fitted values ​​are close to zero, indicating that the model predicts well for low flow values. However, as the fitted value increases, the residuals begin to show greater variability, with some significant deviations observed. This pattern suggests that the model may have difficulty accurately predicting larger flows, potentially due to unaccounted for variability or nonlinear relationships not captured by the current model.

Moreover, these findings suggest that, while the model performs reasonably well for low to moderate flow predictions, there is room for improvement to better predict higher flow values.

  • Histogram of Residuals:

The Histogram of Residuals visualizes the difference between the actual and predicted values. This plot allows for a quick check of the distribution of the residuals.

Ideally, the residuals should follow a normal distribution centered at 0, indicating that the model error is randomly distributed and the model has a good fit. In this plot, the majority of the residuals are concentrated around 0, indicating that the model performs quite well for most predictions. However, a clear rightward skew is visible, with some large positive residuals extending beyond the 6e-14 range.
